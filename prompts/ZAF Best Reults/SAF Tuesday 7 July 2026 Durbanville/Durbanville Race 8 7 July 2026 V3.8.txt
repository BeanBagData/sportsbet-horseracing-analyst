Part 6: Decision Tree Analysis (Gaps, Errors, Issues)

Dissecting the deterministic decision tree reveals three core logical gaps:

[Is Track Turning?] ───► [Yes] ───► [Is Downhill Bend?] ───► [MISSING GATE]
                                                                    │
                                                                    ├──► Must Apply Non-linear Mass Surcharge
                                                                    └──► Must Apply Apprentice Volatility Tax

1.  Lack of an Altitude/Slope-Grade Node: The decision tree treats turning
    tracks identically, regardless of whether they are flat (e.g., Kenilworth)
    or downhill-sloped (e.g., Durbanville). A sloped turning track demands a
    separate decision node because gravity and lateral centripetal stress scale
    exponentially with the horse's total mass.
2.  Apprentice Claim Sparing Flat-Line Assumption: The tree assumes apprentice
    weight claims are linear mathematical subtractions
    (W_{\text{eff}} = W_{\text{raw}} - \text{Claim}). In reality, apprentice
    claims are non-linear; the physical advantage of carrying less weight is
    often completely wiped out by the rider's poorer spatial pathing and
    cornering on tight tracks.
3.  Over-penalisation of Fresh Sophomore Layoffs: The detraining logic treats
    all 90+\text{ day} layoffs with high detraining penalties. However,
    matured 3-year-olds sprinting fresh require minimal cardiovascular
    base-conditioning to run 1200\text{m} compared to stayers, and they often
    perform at their absolute peak when fresh.

1. Matured-Sophomore Weight Advantage (MSWA) Index
