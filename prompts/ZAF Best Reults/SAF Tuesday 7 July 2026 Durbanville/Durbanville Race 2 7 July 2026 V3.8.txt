2. Concept Overview: The Development of the Active Learning Model

2.1 Concept of Active Learning

  - Definition of Active Learning: Active learning represents an instructional
    paradigm wherein students actively engage with the curriculum through
    multidimensional interactions, shifting their role from passive information
    recipients to active builders of knowledge [2]. This process is centered
    around higher-order thinking (analytical, synthesis, and evaluative
    capacities) rather than rote auditory assimilation [2].
  - Significance of Active Learning: This pedagogical framework is vital for
    cultivating cognitive autonomy, metacognitive self-regulation, and original
    problem-solving abilities [2]. It directly stimulates long-term memory
    structures and equips students with tactical adaptabilities essential for
    volatile socio-technical landscapes.
  - Role of the Instructor: Under the active learning model, the instructor's
    responsibility shifts from an authoritarian knowledge transmitter to an
    orchestrator of learning environments, customising guidance to support
    student development.
  - Active Learning Strategies: Strategies encompass peer-instruction tasks,
    experiential simulations, cooperative problem-solving structures, and
    targeted interactive group reflections designed to reinforce conceptual
    comprehension.

2.2 Pedagogical Frameworks

  - Constructivism: This framework maintains that learners actively construct
    their conceptual understanding by integrating new experiences with prior
    knowledge, rather than absorbing information in a static, pre-packaged
    manner.
  - Experiential Learning Theory: Developed to emphasise the cyclical process of
    learning through concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract
    conceptualisation, and active experimentation.
  - Inquiry-Based Learning: A student-centered approach where learning is driven
    by questioning, investigation, and analysis, prompting students to explore
    real-world problems.

2.3 Structural Class-Type Adaptability

  - Developmental Pathways: Focuses on the identification and cultivation of
    latent cognitive peaks, gradual conceptual maturation, and structural
    support for unexposed or developing student cohorts.
  - Exposed Pathways: Focuses on performance optimisation, weight-to-performance
    assessments, practical field readiness, and the deployment of consolidated
    skills under competitive testing regimes.
