Section II: Reverse-Engineering the Logical Gaps in V414.1

To prevent future misclassifications of high-class resuming horses and
underestimation of weight-concession dynamics, we must isolate and correct three
major systemic blind spots in the V414.1 logic.

1. The Class-Layoff Blind Spot (Official Ratings vs. PVI)

In V414.1, Knowmore was assigned a high Preparation Volatility Index
(\text{PVI} = 4.0) because of a 117-day spell, and a heavy Geometric Efficiency
Score penalty (\text{GES} = 6.4) for drawing barrier 8.

  - The Error: The model treated these penalties as absolute physical drags
    without evaluating the caliber of the opposition.
  - The Reality: In low-grade South African maiden plates, raw class/ability
    frequently overrides wide draws and lack of peak fitness. Knowmore possessed
    an Official Rating (OR) of 76, the highest in the field (3 points clear of
    Winding Power and Royale Jacket at 73).
  - The Connection Gap: There was no mathematical connection between the field's
    rating-class dispersion and the PVI/GES penalty scale. High-class runners
    must receive a "Class Cushion" to absorb environmental/draw disadvantages
    when facing low-quality fields (\theta_{\text{mean}} < 70).

2. Jockey Booking Class Signals (JBCS)

Trainer Alec Laird entered two runners: Knowmore (returning from 117 days off)
and Master Buddy (fit, active runner).

  - The Error: V414.1 ranked Master Buddy (NKE 72.97) ahead of Knowmore
    (NKE 66.07) purely on fitness and draw metrics.
  - The Reality: Laird’s stable jockey, Calvin Habib, chose to ride the resuming
    Knowmore from barrier 8, leaving the fit Master Buddy to journeyman C.
    Maujean.
  - The Connection Gap: Top stable jockeys rarely choose a wider draw and a
    resuming runner unless internal work and stable expectations point to a
    high-performance index. The model lacked a semantic filter to decode
    multi-entry jockey bookings as a direct class/intent signal.

3. Weight-for-Age (WFA) Kinetic Leverage on Dry Turf

Cover Charge (2YO) carried 56kg, receiving a substantial 4kg weight advantage
from the 3YO runners carrying 60kg.

  - The Error: While V414.1 applied a Viscosity Mass Tax (VMT) discount, it did
    not model how weight advantages behave under specific turf compaction
    conditions (\text{MSCI}_{\text{eff}} \ge 0.80).
  - The Reality: On dry, hard turf tracks (such as Turffontein's "Good" rating
    on the day), lighter horses experience significantly lower vertical impact
    energy loss, allowing them to sustain high-velocity cruising speeds longer
    than heavily laden horses.
  - The Connection Gap: The VMT tax model did not scale dynamically with track
    compaction, leading to the under-ranking of a lightweight 2YO who had
    previously displayed highly competitive home-track form (finishing 5th,
    beaten only 2.8L at Turffontein).
